# 🏆 Nobel Prize Data Analysis

An exploratory data analysis (EDA) project examining over a century of Nobel Prize awards to uncover trends, patterns, and potential biases in global recognition.

---

## 📌 Project Overview

The Nobel Prize is one of the most prestigious global honors, awarded annually since 1901 in categories such as Physics, Chemistry, Medicine, Literature, Economics, and Peace.

This project uses data analysis and visualization techniques to explore:

* Distribution of Nobel Prize winners across categories
* Geographic dominance and the rise of the United States
* Gender imbalance in Nobel Prize awards
* Age trends of laureates over time
* Repeat winners and exceptional cases

The goal is to provide a **data-driven perspective** on whether Nobel Prize recognition reflects global diversity—or if disparities exist.

---

## 📊 Key Questions Explored

* Who are the most represented Nobel Prize winners by gender and nationality?
* When did the United States begin to dominate Nobel Prize awards?
* How significant is the gender imbalance among laureates?
* At what age do people typically receive a Nobel Prize?
* Who are the youngest and oldest Nobel Prize winners?
* Which individuals have won the Nobel Prize more than once?

## 📈 Key Insights

* **U.S. Dominance**: The United States emerged as a leading contributor to Nobel laureates starting in the 1930s.
* **Gender Imbalance**: Male laureates significantly outnumber female laureates, though representation has improved in recent decades.
* **Age Trends**: The average age of Nobel Prize winners has increased over time, often reflecting longer research cycles.
* **Repeat Laureates**: A small group of individuals have won multiple Nobel Prizes, highlighting exceptional contributions.
* **Youngest Winner**: Malala Yousafzai became the youngest Nobel laureate in 2014.
